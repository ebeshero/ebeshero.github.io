Fall 2013 Digital Humanities Class at Pitt-Greensburg

Course Projects for Digital Humanities/Digital Studies

Due Dates: Weekly reporting by each group to instuctors, Finals Week Deadline for Projects.

This project involves the class as whole engaged generally in two kinds of activities:

  1. Preparing new files to publish on “Digital Archives and Pacific Cultures”: 18th-century texts connected with Pacific cultural contact, and analysis of their content, and
  2. Writing consistent markup for indexing the whole archive, following and adapting our TEI system and coding guidelines.

In small groups, each of you will be engaged in a range of research and coding activities which you’ll need to break into stages. Your work will involve document analysis, reading and researching about people, places, and events, as well as markup in XML, and transforming that markup to HTML (with SVG or KML), styled with CSS (and, as needed, some Javascript we’ll prepare to help with dynamic activities.)

Prosopography Development: Go to our Prosopography Folder in Box for model lists and detailed instructions on how to prepare this from your persName and placeName tagging.

All Groups will need to run queries on our archive to keep track of and develop lists of people, places, and dates in use in the archive. We’ll need to start with this, and as we proceed, we’d like each group to identify and research specific people and events, and to write brief descriptions to help build a central “prosopography” file. Prosopography is the study of how people are connected by events, and in building an archive we effectively collect lots of prosopography data: In coding TEI archives, we refer to building “personographies,” “placeographies,” as well as bibliographies and lists of events, etc. We need to work on this aspect of our project—and it involves the following:

  • Establishing a searchable list of each distinct person (place, event) that matters in our archive
  • Defining the best spelling of proper names—and referring all variations on that name back to a central standard. We’ll work out a system for doing this, which involves defining a distinct @xml:id in our prosopography list for each distinct person and event. Then we can go back and use that id value in @ref attributes throughout the site to make connections, build links, and make it possible to find that person.
  • We’re at the beginning stage of this process, so your work is groundbreaking here! By the end of our course, we hope to prepare a partial prosopography file for web viewing, and begin linking it to your coding of specific texts.
  • This work is ultimately connected with a Site Overhaul of “Digital Archives and Pacific Cultures”! (Instructors will work on this, with input from groups: As we prepare new material to add, we’ll need to reformat the site (change our CSS etc.) to better connect our files and to accommodate a new system of searching.)
